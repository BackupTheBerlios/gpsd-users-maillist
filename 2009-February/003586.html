Matt Roberds <mattroberds at cox.net>:
> On Thu, 5 Feb 2009, Jim Rainville wrote:
> > I can telnet to port 2947 and I'm able to talk to the device. I can
> > enter commands and get back reasonable responses ('F' gives me the
> > device, 'P' gives me the position, etc) [...] So then I tried using
> > the gpsd test programs (cgps, xgps, etc) but all these programs show
> > no data.
> 
> My initial guess would be along the lines of a DNS/networking problem
> (yes, even for your local machine).  When you used telnet, did you say
> "telnet localhost 2947" or "telnet 192.168.1.3 2947" or something else?
> Did you try giving the same address to cgps/xgps instead of just letting
> it take the default?  In other words, if you said
> "telnet localhost 2947", say "xgps localhost" or "xgps localhost:2947",
> or if you said "telnet 192.168.1.3 2947", say "xgps 192.168.1.3" or
> "xgps localhost 192.168.1.3:2947".  If this makes things start working,
> you might examine your /etc/hosts and maybe your DNS configuration
> ( /etc/resolv.conf among other things).
> 
> Another test you could do would be to point cgps/xpgs at a public gpsd,
> like "xgps gpsd.mainframe.cx:2947", and see if you get any data.  (That
> particular host should give you lat/lon for Sunnyvale, California, USA;
> about 37.4 N and 122.0 W.)  If that doesn't work, then the networking
> configuration on your machine is somehow incorrect.
> 
> Another option would be some kind of permissions thing, but being able
> to get data with telnet makes that slightly less likely.  Still, if you
> have turned on something like SELinux or stricter-than-usual firewall
> rules, that might be another place to look.

Jim, Matt: If you are able to verify that an error in DNS was involved, 
please tell me as I think the above response might be a good candidate 
for inclusion in the FAQ.
