> > while you're at it, you might as well mention that the
> > other "classic" error is a 100-150m shift when using the
> > wrong map datum. e.g. map is in NAD27 while GPS is giving
> > WGS84.

Eric wrote:
> Wil this do?
> 
> <p>If you're using <tt>gpsd</tt> with software that plots your
> position on a map, and you seem to be getting latitude/longitude
> that is at a fixed offset from reality, it is possible the
> base datum of the map is something other than the WGS84 GPS
> uses. An important case of this is North American Geodetic
> Survey maps based on NAD27; you may see a displacement of as
> much as 100-150m with respect to WGS84.</p>


yeah, but there's nothing special about NAD27. pretty much anything
from the 1860s to the 1960s might have an offset in that range.
It's really not until we got into the satellite era that we had
a good estimate of the shape & center of the geoid. After then
it converges pretty quickly on the estimate WGS84 uses. 

probably this is more a usual suspect for GIS mappers than gpsd
navigational users, but the national map projection systems in
some countries are still defined in the old ways and much if
not most software which does on-the-fly map reprojection seems
to get this one wrong..

how about

<p>If you're using <tt>gpsd</tt> with software that plots your
position on a map, and you seem to be getting latitude/longitude
that is at a fixed 100-150m offset from reality, it is possible
that the geodetic datum of the map is something other than WGS84
which GPS uses.</p>
