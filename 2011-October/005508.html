> There is no such thing as a dual freq
> GPS chip.
> 
> You will only find dual freq GPS receivers from high-end
> brands such as NovAtel, Septentrio, NavCom, Leica, etc.
> 
> The challenge for an L1-L2 receiver is tracking the L2 P/Y
> code, which requires a wide-band (>20 MHz) L1 and L2
> front end. If you want any kind of L1-L2 performance, the RF
> design is quite a bit more complicated than your
> standard  L1-only narrow band (2 MHz) receiver. (Note:
> the new L2C modulation does allow a narrow-band design, but
> if you want any code multipath mitigation you would have to
> go wide band anyway).
> 
> On top of that, the market for L1-L2 (survey-level quality)
> is orders of magnitudes smaller than the L1-only market
> (everything else). The result is a fairly expensive product.
> It will be a challenge to find any decent L1-L2 rx for under
> $ 2k.
> 
> Recently, a few leading GPS companies have been able to
> package their L1-L2 products in a relative small package,
> around credit-card size. This pricing hasn't shrunk
> accordingly, unfortunately...
> 
> What performance are you aiming for that you need L1-L2?

I am researching the viability of the development of a low cost reference
station. Currently I am thinking of starting with a miniEclipse II (it's the
cheapest I've been able to find) with L1/L2 and RTK. 
Do you think this board will do the job?
