Chris Kuethe wrote:
> Do you have good skyview? Any tall buildings either obstructing parts
> of the sky or possibly causing reflections?

This is a good question. I have a BU-353 and when using it indoors (our
building is built a bit like Windsor castle) I often see the fix bounce
out by ~100m. We have a solid wall just across from the office window
and a 10 story building across the street. In the field, given a clear
view of the sky the position is stable within 2-5m. I have always
assumed the 100m errors were because of multipath reflections and the
GPS's ability to lock onto those very weak signals. Interestingly, the
100m errors all happen along a very clear bearing from the fixed GPS
antenna.

> > My questions:
> > Is this level of accuracy typical cheap GPS receivers in general,
> > or is this one just a turkey?

If it happens in an urban jungle, count it as reflections causing
delays in the signal arrival times of obscured sats. If it happens with
a clear sky to the horizon, it's a goose.


> "Poor man's DGPS". no. for you to have a hope of having that work,
> you need to have the clocks in your receivers be synchronized, and
> you need to have a way to ensure that they're both making 
> observations at the same time.

And a way to ensure that they are tracking the same birds as they go
above and below the horizon or other barriers.

To do it properly, you need 2 GPSs which output per satellite pseudo-
ranges, and keep one at a surveyed position. Calculate corrections for
base station GPS vs. surveyed position, and (post-processing) apply
corrections to the roving GPS record. Add a real-time VHF link to the
roving GPS with the corrections and you've reinvented DGPS.


> > My intended application involves some (very rough) GIS stuff for
> > educational purposes on a very limited budget,

may I suggest some free solutions then:
GRASS GIS (load GPS data with v.in.gpsbabel or v.in.ascii modules)
  http://grass.ibiblio.org
QuantumGIS (GPS Plugin [also using gpsbabel])
  http://www.qgis.org
GPSdrive  (live gpsd using navigation software)
  http//www.gpsdrive.de


> > and my past experience with (much higher end) GPS survey gear
> > suggested that I could get accuracy to within 2-3 meters and
> > consistency to about a meter (within a few-hour daylight time
> > window), which is "good enough".

You should be able to get that with most mainstream GPSs, as with CD
players, you (mostly) get the same stream of digital output from a $50
or $2000 player. The big difference comes in things like error handling
or hard core precision....
