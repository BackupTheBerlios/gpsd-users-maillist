It's not that dumb a question.  It's actually fairly amazing how much 
GPS technology can do considering how incredibly weak the signals 
are: at the earth's surface, GPS signals are considerably weaker than 
cosmic background noise.  Indeed, the only reason a GPS receiver can 
"hear" the signals at all is that it knows what it expects to hear, 
and just time-shifts its expectations around until it gets an exact 
match.  This is why a receiver can get a lock within seconds if it 
has sufficiently recent information in memory, but can take several 
minutes to get a fix - even with a perfect view of the sky - if it 
has to start from scratch (i.e. not knowing what time it is or what 
satellites it should be able to see).  For the same reason, you may 
find that once your receiver has a fix, it will be able to keep it in 
substantially worse conditions (worse view of the sky, inside glass, 
etc) than what it requires to get the initial fix.

I remember a glider instructor in New Zealand putting about five 
layers of duct tape over the antenna of a GPS unit inside a glider, 
then wondering why it kept losing its fix...
