> Are there genuinely devices available with >10Hz update speed?  I
> haven't heard of such a thing?

Yes. Many are augmented with inertial navigation hardware (INS) often
consisting of gyroscopes and inclinometers. INS devices are very
accurate in the short term. Unfortunately, they drift over time and, of
course, cannot provide absolute locations. Enter the GPS. The better
systems solve all this in a Kahlman filter. Such systems can have a
surprisingly short delay for these calculations (milliseconds).

> > We track a high speed vehicle. In fact, we use the GPS to locate

> > a 10 Hz receiver and 38400 baud (and Linux which can have respectable
> > throughput for these sorts of things) the GPS information allows more
> > accuracy/repeatability than the operator pressing a button.
> 
> This is somewhat confusing description of your system?  You say you have
> a high speed vehicle and are looking for better accuracy than a human
> pressing a button with less than 1m accuracy?  This seems crazy
> difficult for anything other than a very highly skilled operator to even
> approach?  Surely the computer does better nearly all the time?

Indeed it does. That is what I was trying to say. Still, don't
underestimate the accuracy of the human. They are pressing the button at
visible locations (e.g., at the start of an intersection). The main
reason we have moved to GPS triggering is that some environments are now
using non-visible or non-drive-past-able trigger points. This is much
more difficult for the human to react to. Human reaction time is in the
order of a couple milliseconds. Some humans can even be trained better
than a monkey in this sort of task :)=

This is one reason we use RS-232 GPS receivers: they have a
pulse-per-second signal that allows greater connection between the
system time and the time in the NMEA stream, which always lags in
arrival. USB GPS receivers need not apply.
