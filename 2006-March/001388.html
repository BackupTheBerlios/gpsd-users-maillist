On 3/9/06, Eric S. Raymond <esr at snark.thyrsus.com> wrote:
> I'd take a patch that implemented a compile-time option to lock gpsd
> to a fixed baud rate, avoiding autobauding.  Again, this would further
> slim down the daemon for embedded deployments.  I'm much more
> reluctant to implement a runtime option to fix baud rate, for reasons
> already hashed over at length on the dev list.

Sounds like a plan. I'll look into that shortly, though if someone
beats me to it...

> Increasingly, the focus of work on this project seems to be shifting
> away from the daemon core.  One direction is clients. The work on C,
> Python, and now Perl client libraries has borne the expected fruit; we
> now have a rather large assortment of clients, and new ones have
> become quite easy to write.

And this is a good thing. The daemon works, and now people are
learning use it for stuff.

> A third direction is open-source support for flashing GPS firmware.
> Chris Kuethe wrote a pre-alpha SiRF flash utility, and I re-factored
> it into GPS-type-independent and SiRF-specific pieces glued together
> by a driver architecture rather like gpsd's.  As far as I know, this
> code has not been tested.
>
> * Question for Chris Kuethe: Chris, are you listening?  What's our next
> step?

Well, I need few things:
 * permission from SiRF to redistribute their firmware blobs (which I
don't have yet). i have very detailed information regarding the
behaviour of the SiRF receivers during programming, so cooking up an
accurate "test bot" shouldn't be a problem. Firmware distribution is
still a problem.
 * a stash of sacrificial hardware to test on. For the benefit of
everyone else, the reason why the gpsflash manpage has all those dire
warnings about turning your receiver into a brick is because I wreck
two of them while mucking about with new firmwares. It's a tricky
thing.
 * documentation for other receiver types. I don't recall seeing
information for other receivers on how to flash the firmware (not that
it doesn't exist). I sold my garmin, so I can't exactly reverse
engineer their protocol. If other people feel like reverse engineering
some protocol or hounding vendor tech support for documentation before
i get around to it, great. Those feeling really brave are encouraged
to have a look at gpsflash and produce the appropriate routines for
non-sirf hardware.
