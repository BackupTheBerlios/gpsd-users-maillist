Matt Roberds wrote:
> In a small powerboat, you could conceivably just chase the GPS
> heading, while the device tells you "go two degrees right... OK, now
> go three degrees left... OK, three right".  In a sailboat, it helps to
> have more information about the environment, so you can sail in the
> right direction. If you're due west of where you want to be, the GPS
> will tell you "sail along a heading of 90 degrees".  But if the wind
> is straight out of the north, and you try to point the boat due east,
> you're going to end up sailing right past your target on the south
> side.  You've got to sail northeast, in a direction determined by how
> hard the wind is blowing and the performance of your boat... the wind
> speed and direction _is part of_ the navigation solution.  It's nice
> if the information on the wind shows up in the same place as the
> information from the GPS, which is why people might want gpsd to
> handle both.
> 
> An airplane has somewhat similar considerations to a boat. 


Certainly "set & drift" are critically important naviagational data,
especially if GPS coverage becomes poor and things fall back to DR mode.
Speed through the fluid != speed over the ground. As mentioned some time
ago, this has caused a number of mishaps- famously the grounding of the
568-foot cruise liner Royal Majesty on the Rose and Crown shoals near
Nantucket, June 10, 1995.
  http://archive.capecodonline.com/special/andreadoria/capewrecks22.htm

and it's the law to have these naviagational aids available:
  http://www.hklaw.com/Publications/OtherPublication.asp?ArticleID=3702
   (link includes more details on the Royal Majesty disaster)
