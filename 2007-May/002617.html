On 5/7/07, kd4e <kd4e at verizon.net> wrote:
> So, when we are looking for a highly compatible
> GPS does the name Fastrax tell us something
> useful or must we dig down deeper?

Aside from a couple of receivers that are known to emit garbage for
NMEA, pretty much anything that speaks NMEA will work. We prefer
binary protocols with single-packet PVT solutions, of course.

Have a look at http://isuite.fastrax.fi/ - there are some pretty
obvious gaps in the documentation, but the protocol decoders for PVT
and SV info work. No fancy mode control or configuration yet. :P Thus,
anything from Fastrax will have the same level of support, no matter
what hardware they're running isuite on.

> Sure wish we could find a vendor who sells GPS
> units of whose guts we are certain so that we
> may buy with confidence!

The way you do that is organize a group buy, round up a few hundred
thousand dollars, and call the nice folks at some vendor and say
"here's a big box of money, build us receivers that don't suck." It's
all about money - some days prolific controllers are cheaper, some
days the FTDI is. For some applications the Sony/SiRF/uNav chip has
the right price-performance mix. Or buy from a vendor who's not so
concerned with price and is more concerned with a stable
bill-of-materials. This is why we try break the hardware list down the
way we do.

> I don't care about Bluetooth or not, if someone
> made a 5.6GHz interface that is fine as well.
> It is the absence of cables that is valuable -
> and Linux compatibility!

Linux compatibility is also impeded by vendors who aren't terribly
cooperative about documentation or firmware licensing. And by the
crude toolset.

You've obviously never been to a security conference with wireless
net. Absence of cables is a not a guaranteed win. What if I start
flooding the ISM band with noise ... I can DoS your entire rig. With
cables, I can at least have a little more confidence in my data
integrity. For automotive uses, I guess you can make the case that
wireless is a win.
