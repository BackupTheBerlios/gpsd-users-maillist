chris wrote:
 > On Sat, May 24, 2008 at 8:24 AM, Paul Fox <pgf at foxharp.boston.ma.us> wrote:
 > > i've heard a couple of people lately say, effectively, this:
 > >    "Long lock-on times have been reduced considerably in the latest
 > >    generation of cheap GPS hardware.  Initializing with a good guess
 > >    makes it even faster, just a few seconds."
 > >
 > > does gpsd "initialize with a good guess"?  if it doesn't, could it
 > > help?
 > 
 > It doesn't do that, but it could help. Of course doing that may or may
 > not be possible for a given receiver configuration.
 > 
 > When you have some state to load, that's called ... something other
 > than a cold start. In a cold start the receiver has to figure out what
 > time it is, what PRNs are visible, where it is, and some other stuff.
 > Anything you can do to prevent it from having to deduce these things
 > will speed up fix times. Eventually you get to do what SiRF calls a
 > "snap start" - other vendors call it different things - but for that
 > to work you need to know where you are already, you need to have a
 > fresh ephemeris loaded, you need to know the time to about 1ms, and
 > you need to have the onboard oscillator calibrated. It's basically a
 > question of "how fast can you re-acquire if I preload all your
 > internal state?"

thanks.  so i guess the question is, how much would having any
one of these things help?  and, conversely, how much would giving
the gps bad data (e.g., time off by a few seconds) hurt?  and
which of these would give the biggest bang for the buck.
