I am toying with the idea of creating a network layer on top of the
AIS standard, and am trolling for comments and input. The network
would provide low-bandwidth and high-latency communication to ships
offshore, where network access is presently very expensive and slow.

The AIS specification includes definitions of data packets (message
6,7 & 8, see ITU-R, M1371-4) for user-defined binary data. These are
generally unused but are supported by the transceiver chips, even if
most OEMs do not expose the functionality on the surface of the
package. The AIS standard in any case does not include routing; AIS
data communication is ship-to-ship, line-of-sight, single-hop,
tits-on-bull, and as I said, apparently not utilized by anyone (except
for ship location of course).

I believe it is possible, with some clever use of distance vectors and
store-and-forward routing, to build a network layer on these packets.
The network could be bridged to the internet by GSM/GPRS connections
made by ships near shore.

I have heard rumors that routing over AIS is forbidden by the Coast
Guard but have scoured regulations and standards documents without
finding anything definite.

I am seeking comments on feasibility, legality, and possible
intellectual property conflicts. I do have a design in mind but will
keep quiet until the Net Has Spoken.

Speak, Net, speak!
