> Warnings are just warnings, not Errors.  They are warning usually

Sorry, that's wrong.  Here's one common failure scenario for the assumption
you are making that I recall from, um, I think it was the PDP11 (but don't
hold me to that):

1) Your target machine has the alignment requirement that structures
have to begin on a 16-bit boundary (even address), but character
arrays can begin at any byte boundary.  This is actually not uncommon;
on a lot of word-addressed machines dereferencing of character
pointers was compiled to a word fetch followed by shift-and-mask.

2) You declare a character array.  Your compiler, not knowing you're 
going to commit unnatural acts with the array later, space-optimizes by
starting it at an odd address.

3) You cast the start of array to a struct and try to mine out a field
of larger than char size.  

4) You just tried to direct-access two or more contiguous bytes starting on 
an odd address.  If your machine design a silicon doohickey called a barrel 
shifter, this would not be a problem.  As it is....

5)  *BOOM!* Your code fails with a bus error.
