On 9/30/06, Hamish <hamish_nospam at yahoo.com> wrote:
> Chris Kuethe wrote:
> > High sensitivity receivers for indoor positioning are getting
> > interesting and popular - not that long ago I was driving around in an
> > underground parkade in Vancouver with no line of sight to the outside
> > (nevermind skyview) and I still got a fix with my SS3.
>
> just because you get a fix doesn't mean it's a good one. Using weak
> reflected echo signals could easily put your position off by 100m ...

Indeed.

In this case the fixes did actually line up with my location quite
nicely. Which was lucky. Very Lucky. Much like I mentioned on the dev
list, you need to be mindful of your significant figures. If my
estimated positional error is in the tens of meters, it doesn't make
much sense to believe your decimeters.

The point there is that we're now getting very affordable chipsets
that are both good at finding a signal where signal has no rightful
place to be, and then hanging on to it. Mature that technology 3
years, add some dual-frequency support, some more magic I can't think
of right now,  and I think we have sunspot-proof GPS.

Plus, we'll probably have more accessible A-GPS in our phones and more
location-based services. But until such time as I get NMEA out of my
phone, it's just a battery waster.
